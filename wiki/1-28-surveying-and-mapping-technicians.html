Surveying and Mapping Technicians

Job Title: Surveying and Mapping Technicians
Occupation Group: Architecture & Engineering Occupations
Entry-Level Education: High school diploma or equivalent
Median Annual Wage: $49,780 (US, 2023)
Number Employed: ~66,900
10-Year Job Growth: 1% (2022-2032), ~100 new jobs

1. What They Do

Definition: Surveying and Mapping Technicians collect data and make maps of the Earth's surface. Surveying technicians visit sites to take measurements of land features, while mapping technicians (including photogrammetrists and cartographers) use geographic data to create maps.

Typical Daily Tasks

  • Surveying Techs: Operate surveying instruments (total stations, GPS), set up control points, record field notes, and take measurements.
  • Mapping Techs: Use GIS and mapping software to create maps, analyze spatial data, and update geographic databases.
  • Both: Work under the supervision of surveyors, cartographers, or photogrammetrists.

Tools & Tech Stack

  • Machines: Total stations, theodolites, GPS receivers, laser scanners, drones (UAVs).
  • Software: GIS software (ArcGIS, QGIS), CAD software (AutoCAD Civil 3D), photogrammetry software (Pix4D).
  • Programming: Python scripting for GIS automation is a plus.

Common Specializations

  • Land Surveying Technician
  • Geodetic Survey Technician
  • GIS Technician
  • Photogrammetry Technician
  • Cartographic Technician

2. Entry & Growth

How to Become One

  • Education: A high school diploma is the minimum, but an associate's degree in surveying technology or geomatics is often preferred.
  • Certifications: Certified Survey Technician (CST) is a common and valuable certification.
  • Experience: On-the-job training is essential. Experience is gained by working as part of a survey crew.

Key Competencies

  • Hard Skills: Surveying techniques, GPS/GIS operation, data collection, CAD, map reading.
  • Soft Skills: Attention to detail, communication, teamwork, physical stamina (for field work).
  • Behavioral Traits: Enjoys working outdoors, methodical, precise.

Career Pathways

  • Vertical: Instrument Person → Survey Technician → Crew Chief → Licensed Surveyor (with further education and licensure).
  • Horizontal: Specialize in a technology like GIS, LiDAR, or drone mapping.
  • Management vs. Expert: Become a lead technician/crew chief or a specialist in a specific mapping technology.

Learning Resources

  • Certifications: Certified Survey Technician (CST), GISP (for mapping techs)
  • Communities: National Society of Professional Surveyors (NSPS)

Interview & Portfolio

  • Common interview questions: Equipment knowledge, data collection procedures, problem-solving in the field.
  • Portfolio: Sample field notes, maps created, CAD drawings.

3. Market & Trends

Wage Distribution

  • P25: $35,000
  • Median: $49,780
  • P75: $80,000 (varies by location and specialization)

Job Outlook

  • Slow growth, but steady demand from construction, engineering, and land development.
  • Technology is a major driver of change, with GPS, laser scanning, and drones making data collection more efficient. Technicians need to keep their tech skills up to date.

Geographic & Industry Distribution

  • Hot cities: California, Texas, Florida (states with high construction and development activity)
  • Major industries: Engineering and architectural services, government (local, state, federal), construction

Work Environment

  • Surveying Technicians: Spend most of their time outdoors in various weather conditions and terrain. The work can be physically demanding.
  • Mapping Technicians: Work primarily in offices.
  • Both roles require a high degree of precision and accuracy.
Related Occupations: Surveyor, Cartographer, Civil Engineering Technician, Drafter
