1. What They Do

Definition: Surveyors make precise measurements to determine property boundaries and provide data on the shape, contour, location, and elevation of land or land features. Their work is crucial for construction, mapmaking, and real estate development.

Typical Daily Tasks

  • Core: Plan and conduct surveys, establish legal boundaries, prepare survey plats and reports.
  • Collaborative: Work with engineers, architects, construction managers, and lawyers.
  • Supportive: Research historical records, manage survey crews, testify in court cases regarding their findings.

Tools & Tech Stack

  • Machines: Total stations, GPS/GNSS receivers, laser scanners (LiDAR), drones.
  • Software: CAD software (AutoCAD Civil 3D, Trimble Business Center), GIS, data processing software.
  • Programming: Not a primary skill, but some use scripting for data processing.

Common Specializations

  • Boundary Surveyor: Establishes legal property lines.
  • Construction Surveyor: Provides measurements for construction projects.
  • Geodetic Surveyor: Measures large areas of the Earth's surface with high precision.
  • Topographic Surveyor: Creates maps showing the features and elevation of a piece of land.
  • Hydrographic Surveyor: Surveys bodies of water.

2. Entry & Growth

How to Become One

  • Education: A bachelor's degree in surveying or geomatics is typically required for licensure.
  • Certifications: Licensure as a Professional Land Surveyor (PLS) is required to certify legal documents. This involves passing exams and fulfilling experience requirements.
  • Experience: Several years of work under a licensed surveyor are required to become licensed.

Key Competencies

  • Hard Skills: Surveying principles, geomatics, GPS/GIS, CAD, knowledge of property law.
  • Soft Skills: Decision-making, problem-solving, attention to detail, communication.
  • Behavioral Traits: Enjoys a mix of field and office work, precise, responsible.

Career Pathways

  • Vertical: Survey Technician → Surveyor-in-Training → Professional Land Surveyor (PLS) → Principal/Owner of a firm.
  • Horizontal: Specialize in a high-tech area like LiDAR or hydrographic surveying, or move into a role in civil engineering or urban planning.
  • Management vs. Expert: Manage survey projects and teams or become a technical expert and legal authority on boundary issues.

Learning Resources

  • Certifications: Professional Land Surveyor (PLS) license.
  • Books: "Brown's Boundary Control and Legal Principles" by Walter G. Robillard.
  • Communities: National Society of Professional Surveyors (NSPS).

Interview & Portfolio

  • Common interview questions: Legal principles of boundaries, technology use, problem-solving scenarios.
  • Portfolio: Survey plats, legal descriptions, project reports, maps.

3. Market & Trends

Wage Distribution

  • P25: $40,000
  • Median: $65,770
  • P75: $105,000 (Licensed surveyors earn significantly more).

Job Outlook

  • Employment is projected to show little or no change. While new technologies make surveyors more productive, the need for their legal and technical expertise remains, especially in land development and infrastructure projects.
  • Many surveyors are nearing retirement, creating replacement needs.

Geographic & Industry Distribution

  • Hot cities: California, Texas, Florida, Colorado.
  • Major industries: Engineering and architectural services, government, construction.

Work Environment

  • A combination of outdoor fieldwork and indoor office work.
  • Fieldwork can be in diverse environments, from urban centers to remote wilderness, and in all weather conditions.
  • Office work involves data processing, drafting, and preparing legal documents.
