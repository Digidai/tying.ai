Janitors and Building Cleaners

Job Title: Janitors and Building Cleaners
Occupation Group: Building & Grounds Cleaning Occupations
Entry-Level Education: No formal educational credential
Median Annual Wage: $31,990 (US, 2023)
Number Employed: ~2,100,000
10-Year Job Growth: 2% (2022-2032), ~41,800 new jobs

1. What They Do

Definition: Janitors and building cleaners are responsible for maintaining the cleanliness and orderliness of various buildings, including offices, schools, and hospitals. Their work ensures a safe and pleasant environment for occupants.

Typical Daily Tasks

  • Core: Sweeping, mopping, and vacuuming floors; cleaning and stocking restrooms; emptying trash receptacles.
  • Collaborative: Report needs for repairs to management, work with other cleaning staff.
  • Supportive: Perform minor maintenance, such as changing lightbulbs or fixing leaky faucets. May also be responsible for locking up buildings at night.

Tools & Tech Stack

  • Machines: Floor buffers, carpet extractors, industrial vacuum cleaners.
  • Chemicals: Disinfectants, cleaning solutions, floor wax.
  • Other: Mops, brooms, cleaning carts, and other manual cleaning supplies.

Common Specializations

  • School Custodian
  • Hospital Environmental Services Technician
  • Commercial Office Cleaner
  • Industrial Cleaner

2. Entry & Growth

How to Become One

  • Education: No formal education is required. Most learn through on-the-job training.
  • Certifications: Not typically required, but safety training (e.g., OSHA) can be beneficial.
  • Experience: Previous cleaning experience is helpful but not always necessary.

Key Competencies

  • Hard Skills: Knowledge of cleaning procedures and chemical handling, ability to operate cleaning equipment.
  • Soft Skills: Reliability, thoroughness, time management, ability to work independently.
  • Behavioral Traits: Trustworthiness, strong work ethic, physical fitness.

Career Pathways

  • Vertical: Cleaner → Lead Janitor → Custodial Supervisor → Facilities Manager.
  • Horizontal: Specialize in a particular type of cleaning (e.g., floor care, window washing) or start their own cleaning service.

Learning Resources

  • Communities: ISSA (The Worldwide Cleaning Industry Association).
  • Training: On-the-job training is standard. Some employers offer formal apprenticeship programs.

Interview & Portfolio

  • Common interview questions: "How do you prioritize cleaning tasks in a large building?", "Describe your experience with different cleaning chemicals."

3. Market & Trends

Wage Distribution

  • P25: $28,000
  • Median: $31,990
  • P75: $39,000

Job Outlook

  • Overall employment is projected to grow about as fast as the average for all occupations.
  • Many job openings will result from the need to replace workers who leave the occupation each year.
  • Demand for cleaning services is expected to remain steady as buildings will always need to be cleaned and maintained.

Geographic & Industry Distribution

  • Employment is spread across all geographic areas and industries.
  • Major industries: Services to buildings and dwellings, elementary and secondary schools, hospitals.

Work Environment

  • Typically work indoors, but may have some outdoor duties like clearing sidewalks.
  • Work is often done when buildings are empty, such as in the evening or at night.
  • Can be physically demanding, involving a lot of walking, standing, bending, and lifting.
