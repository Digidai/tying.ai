Budget Analysts

Job Title: Budget Analysts
(预算分析师)
Occupation Group: Business & Financial Occupations
Entry-Level Education: Bachelor's degree
Median Annual Wage: $82,270 (US, 2023)
Number Employed: ~55,000
10-Year Job Growth: 2% (2022-2032), ~1,200 new jobs

1. What They Do

Definition: Budget analysts help public and private institutions organize their finances. They prepare budget reports and monitor institutional spending. Their work is essential for ensuring organizations are financially efficient and on track to meet their goals.

Typical Daily Tasks

  • Core: Developing annual budgets, reviewing budget proposals for completeness and accuracy, and monitoring spending to ensure it stays within limits.
  • Collaborative: Work with department managers to develop their budgets, present budget recommendations to senior management.
  • Supportive: Analyze data to determine the costs and benefits of various programs, and forecast future financial needs.

Tools & Tech Stack

  • Software: Advanced spreadsheet software (Microsoft Excel), financial planning and analysis (FP&A) software (e.g., Anaplan, Oracle Hyperion), and data visualization tools.
  • Programming: SQL for database queries and sometimes R or Python for more advanced statistical analysis.

Common Specializations

  • Government Budget Analysis (Federal, State, Local)
  • Corporate Financial Planning
  • Non-profit Budget Management
  • Education Finance

2. Entry & Growth

How to Become One

  • Education: A bachelor's degree is the minimum requirement, typically in finance, accounting, economics, or a related field.
  • Certifications: The Certified Government Financial Manager (CGFM) is a common certification for those in the public sector. A Master's degree can also aid advancement.
  • Experience: Experience in an accounting or financial analysis role is often a prerequisite. Internships with government agencies or large corporations are valuable.

Key Competencies

  • Hard Skills: Financial modeling, statistical analysis, knowledge of budgeting principles and financial regulations.
  • Soft Skills: Strong analytical and quantitative skills, excellent communication, attention to detail, negotiation.
  • Behavioral Traits: Detail-oriented, organized, capable of seeing the big picture while managing details.

Career Pathways

  • Vertical: Junior Budget Analyst → Budget Analyst → Senior Budget Analyst → Budget Director or CFO.
  • Horizontal: Move into other financial roles like financial analyst, management analyst, or accountant.

Learning Resources

  • Communities: Association for Budgeting and Financial Management (ABFM), National Association of State Budget Officers (NASBO).

Interview & Portfolio

  • Common interview questions: "Walk me through the process of creating a budget," "How do you handle a situation where a department's budget request exceeds available funds?"
  • Portfolio: Examples of budget reports or financial models you have developed can be a strong asset.

3. Market & Trends

Wage Distribution

  • P25: $65,000
  • Median: $82,270
  • P75: $100,000

Job Outlook

  • Employment growth is projected to be slower than the average for all occupations.
  • Most job openings will come from the need to replace workers who retire or move to other occupations.
  • Budget analysts will continue to be important for organizations to manage their finances and improve efficiency.

Geographic & Industry Distribution

  • A large percentage of budget analysts work for federal, state, and local governments.
  • Other major employers include educational institutions and healthcare organizations.
  • Jobs are often concentrated in state capitals and major metropolitan areas.

Work Environment

  • Typically work in an office setting.
  • The job can be stressful, with long hours during the preparation and review of budgets.
Related Occupations: Accountant, Cost Estimator, Financial Analyst, Management Analyst.
All these roles require strong quantitative, analytical, and financial skills.
