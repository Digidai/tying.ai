What are Property Appraisers and Assessors?

Property Appraisers and Assessors are professionals who estimate the value of real property (land and the buildings on it). Appraisers typically provide valuation services for sales, mortgages, and investments, working for appraisal firms or as independent contractors. Assessors work for local governments to determine the value of properties for property tax purposes.

What do Property Appraisers and Assessors do?

While their employers differ, the core function of both roles is valuation. Their tasks include:

  • Property Inspection: Visiting properties to measure dimensions, note features, and assess the physical condition of the buildings and land.
  • Data Collection: Gathering data on comparable property sales, property characteristics, zoning regulations, and market trends.
  • Valuation Analysis: Using various methods, such as the sales comparison approach, cost approach, and income approach, to determine a property's value.
  • Report Preparation: Documenting their findings in detailed appraisal reports for clients or in assessment records for the government.
  • Record Keeping: Maintaining accurate records of all properties they have valued, including photos and supporting data.
  • Defending Valuations: Appraisers may need to defend their valuations in legal settings, while assessors may meet with property owners to explain their tax assessments.

What are the skills of a Property Appraiser and Assessor?

To provide accurate and defensible valuations, these professionals need a specific skill set:

  • Analytical Skills: The ability to analyze various data points and market information to arrive at a logical estimate of value.
  • Attention to Detail: Meticulousness is required when inspecting properties, gathering data, and preparing reports.
  • Mathematical Skills: Strong math skills are necessary for calculations related to property valuation methods.
  • Problem-Solving Skills: The ability to resolve discrepancies in data and address challenges in valuing unique properties.
  • Time Management: Good organizational skills to manage multiple appraisal assignments or a large portfolio of properties for assessment.

How to become a Property Appraiser or Assessor?

A bachelor's degree is often required, especially for commercial appraisal. All states require appraisers to be licensed or certified, which involves completing specific coursework, passing an exam, and accumulating a certain number of hours of supervised experience. Assessors may have similar requirements set by the state or local government. Continuing education is mandatory for both professions to maintain licensure and stay current with market practices and regulations.
