What are Tax Professionals?

Tax Examiners, Collectors, and Revenue Agents are civil servants who work for government agencies like the Internal Revenue Service (IRS) or state and local tax authorities. They are responsible for the critical function of ensuring that tax obligations are met by individuals and businesses, which in turn funds public services.

What do these Tax Professionals do?

Although they work together to administer the tax system, their roles are distinct:

  • Tax Examiners: They review tax returns filed by individuals and businesses to ensure they are complete and accurate. They check for mathematical errors and verify that deductions and credits claimed are legitimate.
  • Tax Collectors: They are responsible for collecting delinquent taxes. They contact taxpayers who have overdue tax bills, arrange for payment plans, and, if necessary, initiate enforcement actions like placing liens on property or garnishing wages.
  • Revenue Agents: They conduct audits of more complex tax returns, typically for large businesses and corporations. They perform detailed examinations of financial records to verify compliance with tax law and may investigate cases of suspected tax fraud.

What are the skills of a Tax Professional?

These roles require a strong understanding of accounting principles and tax law, as well as specific personal attributes:

  • Analytical Skills: The ability to analyze complex financial data and interpret intricate tax laws and regulations.
  • Attention to Detail: Meticulousness is essential for reviewing detailed financial records and tax forms for accuracy.
  • Communication Skills: Clear communication skills are needed to explain tax issues to taxpayers and their representatives. Collectors, in particular, need strong negotiation and persuasion skills.
  • Integrity: A high degree of honesty and integrity is crucial for handling sensitive taxpayer information and upholding the law.
  • Mathematical Skills: Proficiency in math and accounting is fundamental to all aspects of the job.

How to become a Tax Professional?

A bachelor's degree in accounting is the most common educational background for these positions. Specific coursework in accounting, business law, and finance is highly desirable. For revenue agent positions, the IRS typically requires at least 30 semester hours of accounting courses. New hires undergo extensive on-the-job training to learn specific procedures and tax codes. Strong analytical skills and a high level of integrity are essential personal qualities for success in this field.
