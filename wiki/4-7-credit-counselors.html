Credit Counselors

Job Title: Credit Counselors
Occupation Group: Business & Financial Occupations
Entry-Level Education: Bachelor's degree often preferred
Median Annual Wage: $47,840 (US, 2023)
Number Employed: ~28,000
10-Year Job Growth: 3% (2022-2032), ~900 new jobs

1. What They Do

Definition: Credit counselors provide guidance and support to individuals and families who are having financial difficulties. They help clients manage their debt, improve their financial health, and develop sustainable habits through budgeting and financial education.

Typical Daily Tasks

  • Core: Meeting with clients to review their income, expenses, and debts. Developing personalized debt management plans (DMPs) and budgets.
  • Collaborative: Negotiating with creditors on behalf of clients to arrange for lower interest rates or more manageable payment terms.
  • Supportive: Providing financial literacy education through workshops or one-on-one sessions. Maintaining client records.

Tools & Tech Stack

  • Software: Customer Relationship Management (CRM) software, debt management software, and budgeting applications.

Common Specializations

  • Debt Management Counseling
  • Bankruptcy Counseling
  • Student Loan Counseling
  • Housing and Mortgage Counseling

2. Entry & Growth

How to Become One

  • Education: A bachelor’s degree in a field like finance, accounting, or social work can be helpful. However, some employers may hire candidates with a high school diploma and relevant experience.
  • Certifications: Certification is a standard requirement in the industry. The National Foundation for Credit Counseling (NFCC) offers various certifications, such as Certified Consumer Credit Counselor.
  • Experience: Experience in financial services, social work, or counseling can be advantageous.

Key Competencies

  • Hard Skills: Strong understanding of personal finance, credit, and debt. Budgeting and negotiation skills.
  • Soft Skills: Excellent interpersonal and communication skills, empathy, problem-solving, and patience.
  • Behavioral Traits: Non-judgmental, trustworthy, and a genuine desire to help others.

Career Pathways

  • Vertical: Credit Counselor → Senior Counselor → Counseling Supervisor or Manager.
  • Horizontal: Move into financial education, program development, or related roles like loan officer or personal financial advisor.

Learning Resources

  • Communities: National Foundation for Credit Counseling (NFCC), Financial Counseling Association of America (FCAA).

Interview & Portfolio

  • Common interview questions: "How would you help a client who is overwhelmed with credit card debt?", "Describe your approach to financial education."

3. Market & Trends

Wage Distribution

  • P25: $40,000
  • Median: $47,840
  • P75: $60,000

Job Outlook

  • Employment is expected to grow about as fast as the average for all occupations.
  • Demand for credit counseling is often tied to the overall health of the economy. High levels of consumer debt can increase the need for these services.
  • Many counselors work for nonprofit credit counseling agencies.

Geographic & Industry Distribution

  • Most jobs are in the credit intermediation and social assistance industries.
  • Positions are available nationwide, often through nonprofit agencies, credit unions, and some for-profit companies.

Work Environment

  • Typically work in an office or call center environment.
  • The work can be emotionally demanding, as clients are often in stressful financial situations.
Related Occupations: Personal Financial Advisor, Loan Officer, Social and Human Service Assistant.
These roles all involve providing guidance and assistance to individuals on personal or financial matters.
