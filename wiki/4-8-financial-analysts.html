Financial Analysts

Job Title: Financial Analysts
(金融分析师)
Occupation Group: Business & Financial Occupations
Entry-Level Education: Bachelor's degree
Median Annual Wage: $99,760 (US, 2023)
Number Employed: ~375,000
10-Year Job Growth: 8% (2022-2032), ~28,300 new jobs

1. What They Do

Definition: Financial analysts guide businesses and individuals in making investment decisions. They analyze financial data, study economic and business trends, and prepare reports and recommendations to support these decisions. Their work is fundamental to investing and financial management.

Typical Daily Tasks

  • Core: Building financial models, performing valuation analysis (e.g., DCF, comparable companies), and writing research reports.
  • Collaborative: Presenting investment recommendations to portfolio managers or clients, meeting with company management.
  • Supportive: Monitoring market trends, reading financial news, and maintaining financial databases.

Tools & Tech Stack

  • Software: Advanced Microsoft Excel, Bloomberg Terminal, FactSet, Capital IQ.
  • Programming: Python and R are increasingly used for data analysis and building complex models. SQL is used for database management.

Common Specializations

  • Buy-Side Analyst: Work for institutional investors like mutual funds, hedge funds, and pension funds.
  • Sell-Side Analyst: Work for investment banks and brokerages, providing research to the public.
  • Corporate Finance Analyst: Work within a company to help with capital budgeting and financial planning (often called FP&A).
  • Credit Analyst: Focus on the creditworthiness of companies or governments.

2. Entry & Growth

How to Become One

  • Education: A bachelor's degree in finance, economics, accounting, or a related field is the standard. A strong academic record from a reputable university is often expected.
  • Certifications: The Chartered Financial Analyst (CFA) designation is the gold standard in the industry and is highly respected.
  • Experience: Internships in finance are crucial for breaking into the field.

Key Competencies

  • Hard Skills: Financial modeling, valuation, accounting, statistical analysis, strong quantitative abilities.
  • Soft Skills: Analytical thinking, communication, attention to detail, decision-making under pressure.
  • Behavioral Traits: Competitive, driven, curious, and able to work long hours.

Career Pathways

  • Vertical: Analyst → Associate → Portfolio Manager or VP of Finance.
  • Horizontal: Move from sell-side to buy-side, transition into private equity, venture capital, or corporate development.

Learning Resources

  • Communities: CFA Institute, Wall Street Oasis (online forum).
  • Books: "Investment Banking" by Rosenbaum & Pearl, "The Intelligent Investor" by Benjamin Graham.

Interview & Portfolio

  • Common interview questions: Technical questions on accounting and valuation, stock pitches, "brain teaser" questions, and case studies.
  • Portfolio: A portfolio of stock pitches or financial models can be very effective.

3. Market & Trends

Wage Distribution

  • P25: $75,000
  • Median: $99,760
  • P75: $135,000+ (bonuses can be substantial)

Job Outlook

  • Employment is projected to grow faster than the average for all occupations.
  • Demand is driven by the growing complexity of financial products and the need for in-depth investment analysis.
  • Big data and new analytical tools are changing the profession, requiring analysts to have stronger tech skills.

Geographic & Industry Distribution

  • Jobs are highly concentrated in major financial centers like New York City, Chicago, Boston, and San Francisco.
  • The finance and insurance sector is the largest employer.

Work Environment

  • High-pressure, fast-paced office environment.
  • Long hours, including evenings and weekends, are very common, especially in investment banking.
Related Occupations: Accountant, Personal Financial Advisor, Actuary, Economist.
These roles all require strong analytical and quantitative skills applied to business and finance.
