What is a Community Health Worker?

A Community Health Worker (CHW) is a trusted, frontline public health professional who typically comes from the community they serve. They act as a crucial bridge between residents and the healthcare and social service systems, helping to reduce barriers to care, improve health outcomes, and promote health equity. They are advocates, educators, and navigators for their community members.

What does a Community Health Worker do?

CHWs perform a wide range of activities to support community health. Their responsibilities often include:

  • Health Education: Providing basic health education and information on topics like chronic disease management, nutrition, and maternal health.
  • Care Coordination: Helping individuals navigate the complexities of the healthcare system, including scheduling appointments and arranging transportation.
  • Social Service Linkage: Connecting community members with essential resources such as food assistance, housing support, and insurance enrollment.
  • Advocacy: Voicing the needs of the community to healthcare providers and policymakers to improve service delivery.
  • Outreach: Conducting home visits and community outreach to engage individuals who may be disconnected from traditional healthcare.
  • Support: Offering informal counseling, social support, and encouragement to help individuals manage their health.

What are the skills of a Community Health Worker?

The effectiveness of a CHW relies heavily on their interpersonal skills and their deep connection to the community. Key skills include:

  • Communication and Interpersonal Skills: The ability to listen actively, build trust, and communicate effectively with diverse populations.
  • Empathy and Compassion: A genuine desire to help others and an understanding of the cultural and social factors affecting health.
  • Advocacy Skills: The ability to speak up for the needs and rights of their clients and community.
  • Organizational Skills: Good organizational skills to manage a caseload of clients and track follow-ups and referrals.
  • Knowledge of Community Resources: A comprehensive understanding of the local healthcare and social service resources available.

How to become a Community Health Worker?

Educational requirements for CHWs vary, with many positions emphasizing experience over formal degrees. A high school diploma is often the minimum requirement. Formal education in public health, social work, or a related field can be beneficial. Many CHWs receive on-the-job training and may pursue certification, which is becoming more common and is offered by various state and professional organizations. The most important qualification is often a deep understanding of and trusted relationship with the community being served.
