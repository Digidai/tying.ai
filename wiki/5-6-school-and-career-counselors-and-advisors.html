What are School and Career Counselors and Advisors?

School and Career Counselors and Advisors are professionals who work in educational settings to support students' academic achievement, personal and social development, and career exploration. School counselors typically work in K-12 schools, while career counselors or advisors may work in high schools, colleges, or private practice, focusing specifically on helping individuals make decisions about their educational and professional paths.

What do School and Career Counselors and Advisors do?

These professionals are integral to the educational system, guiding students toward success. Their duties include:

  • Academic Support: Helping students with course selection, study skills, and strategies to overcome academic challenges.
  • Personal and Social Counseling: Providing individual and group counseling to help students with issues like bullying, stress, and developing social skills.
  • Career Guidance: Administering career assessments and helping students explore occupations and postsecondary education options.
  • College and Postsecondary Planning: Assisting students with college applications, financial aid forms, and preparing for entrance exams.
  • Collaboration: Working with parents, teachers, and administrators to create a supportive learning environment for all students.
  • Crisis Intervention: Providing support and intervention for students facing personal crises or emergencies.

What are the skills of a School and Career Counselor?

To effectively guide students, counselors need empathy, strong listening skills, and a deep knowledge of educational and career pathways:

  • Interpersonal Skills: The ability to build rapport and trusting relationships with students from diverse backgrounds.
  • Listening Skills: The ability to listen actively and empathetically to students' concerns and aspirations.
  • Communication Skills: Clear communication skills to work effectively with students, parents, and school staff.
  • Problem-Solving Skills: The ability to help students identify their challenges and develop actionable plans to address them.

    • A master's degree in school counseling or a related field like educational psychology is typically required. Most states require school counselors to be licensed or certified, which may involve completing a supervised internship, passing an exam, and sometimes, having prior teaching experience. Career counselors may pursue credentials like the Certified Career Counselor (CCC) to demonstrate their expertise.
