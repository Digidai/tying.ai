What is a Computer and Information Research Scientist?

Computer and Information Research Scientists are the visionaries of the digital world. They don't just use technology; they invent it. They design new approaches to computing technology and find innovative applications for existing technology. They study and solve complex problems in computing for business, science, medicine, and other fields, pushing the boundaries of what's possible.

What do they do?

These scientists work on the cutting edge, often in academic institutions, government labs, or private R&D departments. Their work involves:

  • Exploring Theory: Investigating fundamental questions about the nature of computation and developing new theories and models.
  • Designing Algorithms: Creating and analyzing algorithms to solve complex problems and improve the efficiency of computer systems.
  • Developing New Technologies: Inventing new hardware, software, and programming languages. This can include research in areas like artificial intelligence, robotics, and computer vision.
  • Publishing Research: Writing and publishing papers to share their findings with the academic and scientific community.
  • Collaboration: Working with scientists and engineers from other disciplines to apply computational techniques to their research.

What are their skills?

This is a highly intellectual field that demands deep technical expertise and creativity. Key skills include:

  • Analytical Skills: The ability to analyze complex systems and problems with scientific rigor.
  • Mathematical Aptitude: A strong foundation in mathematics, especially in areas like discrete math, calculus, and linear algebra, is essential for theoretical work.
  • Problem-Solving Skills: Creativity and persistence in tackling complex and often unsolved computational problems.
  • Critical Thinking: The ability to think logically and systematically to evaluate different approaches and solutions.
  • Technical Writing: Excellent writing skills to clearly document research and publish findings.

How to become one?

A Ph.D. in computer science or a related field is almost always required for positions as a research scientist. A master's degree might be sufficient for some applied research and development roles in private industry. The path involves intensive study, original research, and contributing new knowledge to the field, often culminating in a dissertation. Strong programming skills and a deep theoretical understanding are essential.
